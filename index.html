Kohei Saito and the ecological critique of Karl Marx

Esteban Mercatante

Kohei Saito has been tracing, in the published works, manuscripts and notes of Karl Marx, the evolution of his thought on the way in which the development of the capitalist mode of production produces profound alterations in the metabolism between society and nature.

With the 2017 publication of Karl Marx's Ecosocialism: Capital, Nature, and the Unfinished Critique of Political Economy , which was launched in Spanish as La naturaleza contra el capital. Karl Marx's Ecosocialism and currently having its first edition in Argentina by Ediciones IPS, Kohei Saito quickly became an inescapable reference in Marxist discussions on ecology and capitalism. In Japan, his book Capital in the Anthropocene became a best seller , reaching half a million copies sold in 2020.

In his research, Saito set out to systematize the evolution of Marx's thought on the relationship between social metabolism and natural metabolism, giving great relevance to the unpublished notebooks for the first time in this reconstruction. These works, which have been made known –for now in their original language in German– with the new editions of MEGA(Marx and Engels Gesamtausgabe, that is, Complete Works of Marx and Engels), of which Saito is one of those responsible, include a large number of notebooks of drafts and reading notes made during the last years of Marx's life. During this period, the German revolutionary would have given increasing importance to the study of different natural sciences, as can be seen from the books reviewed. The fundamental interest of these investigations, in Saito's opinion, was in establishing how the development of capitalism altered the balance with the natural metabolism.

In 2022 Saito published Marx in the Anthropocene in English. Towards the Idea of ​​Degrowth Communism , which takes up the thread of his previous works, specifically the aforementioned Capital in the Anthropocene . But the center of his proposal, on this occasion, is found in the character of the communist project that Marx would have come to (re) formulate in the last years of his life, which Saito defines as a degrowth communism. Next we are going to go through central aspects of Saito's work, and we will discuss some points of his view on Marx, Marxism and communism.

unpublished marx

The novel aspect of Saito's work is a deep and systematic study of Marx's unpublished notebooks. During the years from 1868 to 1883, in which Marx completed no less than a third of the notebooks he made throughout his life, the presence of topics related to physiology and other natural sciences, Saito points out, is preponderant. . It would be a sample of the relevance that Marx gave to the ecological question and how it is affected by capitalism. This was not reflected in published texts, because in that period Marx did not complete any new work for the press, beyond the changes introduced to translations or reprints of Capital., some of which Saito highlights as the result of these new theoretical concerns. Saito ventures that the delay in finishing volumes II and III of Capital , the conclusion of which was finally entrusted to Engels, is due to this profound reconsideration of the place of the ecological question in his theoretical edifice, which led him to profoundly reconsider the scheme of his critique of political economy. If Marx had come to capture his new reflections, Saito maintains, he would have given a much more privileged place to ecological criticism. Hence the great value of the notebooks, which provide, in his opinion, a way of approaching the idea that Marx had of how Capital should be completed , very different –says the author– from what Engels captured.

The concept of metabolism

In the reconstruction of Marx's thought, Saito gives a central place to the concept of metabolism . This allowed Marx “not only to understand the universal and transhistorical natural conditions of human production, but also to investigate its radical historical transformations during the development of the modern production system and the growth of the productive forces”  [ 1 ] .

The notion of metabolism, arising from chemistry and physiology, became very popular during the first half of the 19th century. The concept was originally developed to account for the physical and chemical processes of organisms that convert or use energy. These complex interrelated processes are the basis of life on a molecular scale and allow the various activities of cells: grow, reproduce, maintain their structures and respond to stimuli, among others. Prior to Marx, there are precedents for the extension of the concept of metabolism "to philosophy and political economy to describe the transformations and exchanges between organic and inorganic substances, through the process of production, consumption and digestion, both at the both of individuals and of species”  [2 ] .

A direct reference for Marx was Justus von Liebig, a chemist whose studies on the degradation of soil nutrients produced by capitalist agriculture were closely studied by Marx. But Saito underlines the "relative independence of Marx in the use of the concept of metabolism", since, although "Liebig's contribution to the development of the theory of metabolism in Marx is undeniable, the Grundrisse also confirm that he did not simply adhere to Liebig's concept of metabolism”  [ 3 ] .

In the case of Marx, we can find three dimensions in which the concept operates, which can be observed both in the Grundrisse and in Capital : "metabolic interaction between humans and nature", "metabolism of society" and "metabolism of nature”  [ 4 ] .

Following John Bellamy Foster  [ 5 ] , Paul Burkett, and other authors, Saito articulates this theory of metabolism with the idea that the irrationality of capital, which is characterized by alienated relationships within society and in the relationship with the nature, inevitably leads to a metabolic breakdown. This notion appears mentioned twice in Capital , linked in both cases to the effects of population concentration in urban areas due to the development of large industry, and the advance of capital in agriculture. Marx, following Liebig, discusses how the large land ownership that produces capitalist agricultural reconfiguration,

…reduces the agricultural population to a constantly diminishing minimum, opposing it with a constantly increasing industrial population, crowded into the cities; in this way it engenders conditions that cause an unhealable tear in the continuity of the social metabolism, prescribed by the natural laws of life, as a consequence of which the strength of the soil is squandered, a squandering that, by virtue of commerce, takes a lot of money. beyond the borders of the country itself  [ 6 ] .

Foster, and Saito agrees on this, considers that Marx developed a systematic vision of the tendency of capitalism to produce this "unhealable tear" between social metabolism and natural metabolism at different levels. In our opinion, the idea of ​​a systematic development of this approach in Marx is debatable. There is plenty of evidence that Marx became increasingly interested in the ecological problem as part of his critique of political economy, and his method is the basis for a profound ecological critique today, such as that being developed by Foster, Burkett, Saito, Malm and many other authors. This does not mean that this systematic critique of this dimension is deployed in Capital or in the unpublished works of Marx.

For Saito, the concept of metabolism is key to Marx's methodology. He allows her to avoid ontological dualism (considering nature and society as completely separate entities), defending an ontological monist position. "The basic idea of ​​Marx's theory of metabolism is [...] that humans always produce as part of nature and that their activities become increasingly intertwined with extra-human nature in the course of capitalist development"  [ 7 ] . But monism in Marx does not mean considering that it is an undifferentiated unit. “A critical analysis of this social power inevitably requires separating the social and the natural respectively as independent fields of inquiry and analyzing their intertwining thereafter” [ 8 ] . The distinction is necessary because "capitalist social relations "exercise an alien power over reality"  [ 9 ]. Saito contrasts this methodology of Marx with the extreme monism, without differentiations, that he finds in contemporary authors such as Bruno Latour, or, in the eco-Marxist camp, Jason Moore. Saito argues that, through the notion of metabolism, Marx is capable of developing a "methodological dualism", which starts from the ontological unity (society is part of nature and not an independent entity) but separates the levels of analysis, to capture the interactions between the spheres of nature and society, which make up a differentiated unit. In his proposition of an ontological monism and methodological dualism, Saito follows Andreas Malm, who defends what he defines as a "monism of substance with dualism of properties".

Although Saito's reflection on the concept of metabolism in relation to Marx's method may be suggestive, there are two warnings that arise in this regard. The first is that it may be somewhat forced to shift the entire discussion of method and Marxist epistemology into the category of metabolism. One runs the risk of missing out on part of the richness and complexity of all the development of categories that Marx sought to produce in his effort to reproduce concrete reality in thought. The second is that the category of methodological dualism is debatable. It may be more precise –although it is not necessarily a question of opposing positions, far from it– to point out, as other scholars point out to define how Marx understood the relationship of unity/differentiation to which Saito points, that,emergent materialism . This means understanding that reality is articulated in different levels of complexity and hierarchy, within which specific properties can emerge that distinguish, without separating, different subsystems or levels of reality.

Metabolism, after Marx, a missing link?

Few Marxists would have been able, according to Saito, to understand the importance of metabolism in Marx's theory. In Marx in the Anthropocene , he rescues István Mészáros, who popularized the notion of sociometabolism in his reading of Capital , while highlighting Marx's analysis of the relationship with nature. In a similar vein, Saito points out that Luxemburg also gave importance to the concept of metabolism, stating that "the accumulation of capital is a metabolism process that occurs between the capitalist and pre-capitalist modes of production"  [ 10 ] .

Another key piece in the recovery of the concept of metabolism is found for Saito in György Lukács. The discussion starts from the rather famous footnote in History and class consciousness, in which the Hungarian writer affirmed that the dialectical method can only be applied to society, and its expansion does not correspond to nature. With his criticism and his sharp separation of the methods for the study of nature and society, this approach of Lukács is traditionally considered as what Saito, following other authors, considers a characteristic feature of "Western Marxism", or the main stream of it, since Saito recognizes that it is a broad and heterogeneous category: the sharp separation between the methods applicable to the study of nature and society, proposing an exclusive focus on the latter and relegating the study of nature. Separating from the mechanism that characterized Stalinism, the Western Marxists, or some of its main exponents, [ 11 ] . But they did so in such a way as to "completely exclude the sphere of nature and the natural sciences from Marx's social philosophy"  [ 12 ] .

History and Class Consciousness generated heated debate and criticism. In an unpublished work, Tailism and Dialectic , Lukács would have clarified his approach, giving it a very different meaning from the sharp separation between the sphere of nature and that of society. It is here where the concept of metabolism appears, absent in History and class consciousness .

…Lukács insisted that the concept of “metabolism” is indispensable for a correct understanding of the key theme of History and Class Consciousness , namely, avoiding the ontological dualism of Nature and Society and the one-sided approach to society. This one-sidedness is exactly the consequence Western Marxism fell into by ignoring Marx's concept of "metabolism"  [ 13 ] .

Engels vs. Marx?

In Saito's opinion, one of the great people responsible for keeping Marx's ecological thinking hidden for so long is none other than his great friend, editor and co-author of so many classic works, Friedrich Engels. Although Engels was involved early in the study of nature, and reflected already in The situation of the working class in Englandreflections on the devastating effects of the accumulation of capital on the environment (whose effects fell especially on the working class), his gaze would have been very different from that developed by Marx. The latter, moreover, would have developed, in Saito's opinion, a much more informed reading than that of Engels in numerous scientific debates during the last years of his life. Despite the fact that Engels would be aware of this, Saito observes, he continued to affirm after Marx's death (in 1883) that he recognized that, of the two of them, Engels was the expert on these subjects. In the second edition of Anti-DühringIn 1885, Engels claimed that Marx “only gradually, intermittently, and sporadically” followed the rapid development of the natural sciences, despite the fact that, Saito observes, his first-hand knowledge of all the manuscripts Marx had left behind had to be reveal to you Marx's advances in this field.

Among the differences in approach that Saito wants to accentuate between the authors, he observes that the idea of ​​"nature's revenge" that Engels proposes to refer to the blind forces that can be unleashed by intervention on nature is not found in Marx, resulting in unexpected outbreaks that can endanger certain social formations, or even humanity as a whole. Despite this warning from Engels, Saito finds in his statement, in Dialectic of Natureand in other works, the idea that knowledge of the objective laws of nature has a practical meaning, which in his opinion is none other than the "dominion" and "control" of nature. In order to reach the kingdom of “freedom”, it is necessary for humanity to become the “real and conscious master” of nature  [ 14 ] .

But where a formidable distance between Marx and Engels opens up for Saito is in the notion of metabolism. Saito notes that Engels "did not appreciate Liebig's theory of metabolism." In fact, “in Dialectic of Nature he referred to Liebig's concept of metabolism in the context of criticizing him as a 'dilettant' in biology”  [ 15 ] . Although Engels shared Liebig's theory of robbery agriculture, he rejected his vitalist vision, "with its separation of biology from chemistry and its inexplicable principles that are supposed to be unique to living beings" [  16 ] .. Liebig considered that the origin of organic life could not be due to evolution from inorganic matter, but accepted the hypothesis that "eternal life" had been "imported" to the planet from universal space [17  ] , a notion against which “Engels correctly argued –says Saito– that life is the metabolic process that historically arose and evolved from inorganic non-life”  [ 18 ]. Saito acknowledges, as we see, that Engels was correct in his criticism of Liebig. But at the same time, he observes that they negatively influenced Engels' ability to evaluate the contributions of the concept of metabolism, and especially to appreciate Marx's appropriation of it, which was not exclusively dependent on Liebig.

This rejection by Engels of Liebig's concept of metabolism would have had important consequences, in Saito's opinion. At the time of reconstructing the third volume of Capital from Marx's manuscripts, Engels would not have been able to grasp Marx's independent appropriation of the concept of metabolism, which went far beyond the limited meaning of the German chemist. His rejection of Liebig's theory, and not simply a matter of making the text more accessible, Saito ventures, would have prompted him to introduce modifications to Marx's original text. Thus, he shows us that the original text of the famous metabolic fracture passage, which we quoted above, was originally worded as follows:

[In] this way [large-scale land ownership] produces conditions that cause an irreparable break in the interdependent process between social metabolism and natural metabolism prescribed by the natural laws of the soil. The result of this is a waste of the vitality of the soil, and trade carries this devastation well beyond the limits of a single country  [ 19 ] .

Saito himself acknowledges that Engels' modification “may appear subtle”  [ 20 ] . But he nevertheless carries out a symptomatic reading of said modification.

The concealment of the discovery of the ecological Marx that was revealed in Marx's late notebooks, the difficulty in capturing and capturing in his edition/writing of Capital the new ideas developed by Marx on ecological criticism, and the correction decisions that deformed the Marx's thought, make Engels, for Saito, largely responsible for the fact that the “ecological” Marx remained hidden during the 20th century.

Saito's argument about Engels' responsibilities in overshadowing Marx's ecological thought does not appear to be very solid. Although his study of the unpublished notebooks and reading notes contributes to show new aspects of Marx's thought in the field of ecological criticism, which were not known before and which may exhibit some greater or lesser nuance with respect to Engels' view of these themes, gives Engels, as a posthumous editor, inordinate abilities to misrepresent Marx's work. We believe that this operation is inseparable from the very evolution of Marx's thought that Saito wants to find from the new MEGA findings .

Marx vs. Marx?

In Nature Against Capital , Saito did not identify an abrupt break in Marx's thought. But in his most recent work, Marx in the Anthropocene , the Japanese researcher argues that there was a pronounced break or turn in Marx's reflection on history, development, and ecology, which became accentuated above all after 1868, is that is, when the first edition of Capital had already appeared .

Unlike his previous work, Saito does not hesitate here to describe the “first Marx” –a period that he ends up extending until almost the end of his life, although he points out different instances of evolution and self-criticism– without more than “Promethean”, “ Eurocentric”, “ethnocentric”, “productivist”. He even falls into the anachronism of locating Marx as an accelerationist because of one of his proposals, when it comes to a current that emerged at the end of the 20th century and gained influence in the last decade.

At the same time that he affirms this, Saito affirms that the late Marx, who appears in his latest drafts as well as in some subtle modifications introduced in the reissues or translations of Capital , would have faced a profound process of self-criticism. Furthermore, he would not only have re-evaluated those aspects of his thought where Eurocentrism, Prometheanism and fervor for the development of the productive forces were evident. It would have led to a profound reassessment of the perspective on the role of the development of the productive forces as the motor of the contradictions that lead to social transformation, and the place that such development should have under communism, an issue that we will address in depth later on.

What changed in such a short time, between Saito's first book and today, so that your perspective on the evolution of Marx's thought is so markedly modified? The reason cannot simply be that the MEGAs reveal an unknown Marx, because as we have seen, what has changed is Saito's view of Marx's already published works, with respect to the evaluation he gave of them in his 2017 work, La nature against capital . In this case, it is not about new documents that reveal something different.

The main reason that we can find in this change in Saito's view of the evolution of Marx's ideas is that it allows him to accentuate, by contrast, the turn made in recent years by the German theoretician. The debates on nature poured into readings and drafts of the last years of life, or the redefinition of the peasant commune and its probable role in the break with capitalism in the countries where it subsisted, based above all on its exchanges of letters with Russian revolutionaries such as the famous reply to Vera Zasulich, which Marx did not send in the end  [ 21 ], are some of the main samples that Saito offers of this “epistemological break” –the reference to Althusser is explicit in Saito– that led Marx to place the metabolic break at the center of his theory, which would become the fundamental contradiction of this mode of production. Already in Nature against Capital , Saito criticized the researcher Lucia Pradella for warning against “a now influential trend in MEGA studies, to search for a 'new Marx'”  [ 22 ] . It is precisely this "new Marx" that for Saito is in his latest notebooks. In Marx in the AnthropoceneThis statement is reinforced with more emphasis. He does it in a somewhat contradictory way, because almost at the end of the tour, he admits that "Marx clearly continued to believe that technological development under capitalism provides the necessary material conditions for the leap towards socialism", that is, he admits that in any case the The turn in this regard was not so pronounced in Marx, beyond the fact that he immediately added that "his dialectical method led him to emphasize more vehemently the negative and destructive side of technologies"  [ 23 ] . We agree with the latter, but it is neither a revelation nor a turn of the "new Marx." In fact, in his youth Marx criticized the Prometheanism of authors such as Proudhon, something documented by John Bellamy Foster in The Ecology of Marx.

As it happens with the statements expressed by Saito regarding Engels, there is not much originality in what he maintains here, which centrally repeats what was stated many times about the exaggeration of the ruptures in Marx's thought, without much foundation (and that in some cases Saito himself refuted in his first book).

Of course, this does not mean minimizing the importance of Marx's own critical view of his own ideas, and the correction to which they were subjected in many cases. His view on some problems, such as the colonial question, or on the metabolism between capital and nature, did not remain unchanged throughout his life. In Nature against Capital , Saito clearly identifies how Marx makes his view of the problem of the productive forces more complex, introducing more concrete determinations; among others, the specification of "capital's productive forces", which Saito considers relevant to highlight that the development of technologies is never neutral, or important categories such as cooperation and real subsumption, which still do not appear in the Grundrisse of 1857-58. It is also clear the reassessment that Marx made about Asian societies, which, in his first elaborations, supported by faulty sources –and perhaps in some prejudices that he gradually got rid of– considered as tending to stagnation and lack of evolution. . This reassessment of the dynamics of non-capitalist formations, coupled with a deeper insight into the “unequal and combined” effects that the subsumption of capitalism was producing throughout the planet, amalgamating capitalist and pre-capitalist relations of production –as when in the epilogue of the second edition of Capital maintains that in Germany “[w]e suffer not only from the living, but also from the dead” [ 24 ] – allowed him to glimpse the possibility that some non-capitalist social relations such as the Russian commune ( mir) could be the basis for a transition to socialism without going through capitalism. These conclusions reached by Marx mark a change from previous positions. But, no matter how partial or problematic some of Marx's formulations or statements may be, which in some cases were critically reviewed and corrected throughout his life, they are not enough to build a "first Marx" loaded with the whole series of negative aspects. that Saito adjudicates him. Marx was a more emphatic critic of colonialism from the end of the 1850s, when he began to raise more emphatically the nefarious role of England in Ireland or India; but this shift in emphasis does not make his previous positions Eurocentric. But all these modifications, in some important cases,Marx in the Anthropocene .

Unlike Saito, it seems to us that a systematic and consistent reading of Marx's elaborations shows that both in his youth and in his last years, the German revolutionary grasped the potentialities created by the capitalist mode of production, at the same time that he was capable of observing –or in most cases rather guessing or anticipating– its darkest derivations. Marx's immanent critique of capitalist modernity always emphasized the need to reappropriate – which also means criticizing and redirecting or reconfiguring – the productive forces of capital. Neither Prometheanism or productivism without more, nor outright rejection. Beyond the evolutions or learning that we can find in Marx, it is difficult to substantiate that he has abandoned this tension. More doubtful, as well as extemporaneous, it turns out to think that Marx may have embraced a “degrowth communism”. Let's look at this issue in more detail.

Productive forces, metabolic fracture and communism

Before delving into the notion of degrowth communism, which he finds in Marx, Saito makes a series of very pertinent criticisms of what he identifies as a revival of utopian thought expressed by various post-capitalist proposals by authors such as Aaron Bastani , Paul Mason , Nick Srnicek and Alex Williams , among others, who with their differences between them coincide in emphasizing technological solutions to the contradictions of this mode of production.

… Promethean ideas once again have a great influence within political ecology. In fact, ecomodernist ideas become hegemonic as the ecological crisis deepens. Now, the development and application of gigantic technologies and science seems to be the only solution that is fast enough and on a sufficient scale to address the serious threat of climate breakdown  [ 25 ] .

Saito dismantles several of the fallacies on which post-capitalist proposals are based. “Fully automated post-capitalism propagates an alternative hope that everyone will continue to drive electronic SUVs, switch smartphones every couple of years, and eat cultured meat burgers,” he says  [ 26 ] .

Saito points out an interesting paradox, which is similar to what we have also criticized on another occasion to these post-capitalist proposals . The author claims that

...hidden under the optimistic tone of this technocratic vision is actually a pessimistic 'capitalist realism', which holds that there is no strong class struggle to challenge existing social relations and fundamentally break away from the capitalist way of life. People are deprived of the power to transform the system, and that is why technology must play a central role in filling the void left by agency  [ 27 ] .

Hence, as we said in our critique , the unfathomable abyss between the horizon of the future that they invite us to embrace and the tasks of the immediate present. We start from a radical look at the possibilities inscribed in the disruption that is already underway. But the way there would be to redouble the commitment to “neo-reformist” strategies. Between post-capitalism and the present, the only clear roadmap is to seek to revive policies of the welfare state, with some innovations such as promoting a universal basic income and other similar measures, but without challenging the power of capital. An "invention" of the future that ends up being quite nostalgic.

Saito concludes that this productivist vision of post-capitalism "ends up endorsing capitalist value standards under the guise of a grandiose emancipatory project for infinite production and consumption." He renounces “the revolutionary subjectivity of the working class and accepts the reified agency of machines as the subject of history”  [ 28 ] .

Now, in the face of these proposals based on a technological fetishism, what communist strategy and perspective can be opposed? Saito, as we anticipated, claims to have discovered in Marx a "degrowth communism." A key link in establishing this perspective would have been in his study of pre-capitalist agricultural communes during the last years of his life.

The commune, whose history throughout Europe – and also in non-European forms – Marx traced during his last years by studying an extensive bibliography, would have revealed to him how social formations based on a balanced, rational metabolism between nature and society could be sustained. , which was opposed to the “unhealable tearing” that capitalism tended to produce. What characterized the agricultural commune, and which Marx ponders much more positively in his mature evaluation than he had in earlier texts, was its tendency to stationary reproduction. That is to say, that these societies were characterized by a reproduction on a scale that was always more or less similar, unlike capitalism with its necessarily expanded reproduction to sustain the accumulation of capital. A stationary reproduction implies a balanced metabolism, since it allows the cycles of waste absorption to be fulfilled –which also responds to the fact that there is no fracture between the countryside and the city– and of nutrient recovery. Saito points out that the draft responses to Zasulich were not an isolated expression, but part of a more global reassessment of the agricultural commune.

In the 1880s, Marx recognized that the persistent stability of communes without economic growth is the underlying basis for achieving sustainable and egalitarian metabolic interaction between humans and nature. This marks a stark contrast to Marx's earlier negative comments on the steady state and invariability of the Asiatic communes in the 1850s and even in Volume I of Capital  [ 29 ] .

Although we already knew Marx's intuition about the possibilities that, determined by the ongoing capitalist globalization, could arise from an “unequal and combined development” –although he could barely intuit this concept that Trotsky would develop several decades later– in Russia, Saito inscribes it in a broader and deeper framework of reflections, and that is new and interesting. The same can be said of the connection that he finds between the investigations that Marx developed on pre-capitalist societies and those of the natural sciences, addressing authors such as Carl Fraas as well as the aforementioned Liebig. The confluence of both agendas in Marx demonstrates, in Saito's opinion, the effort he made to think about the "sustainability" of a future post-capitalist society.

Saito affirms that degrowth communism is a post-scarcity society, that is, it is characterized by abundance of wealth. But it is a wealth that does not simply mean more material goods or higher per capita consumption . Saito relies on a discussion of Marx in the Grundrisse to support another notion of wealth. In that 1857 draft, Marx stated:

…if wealth is stripped of its limited bourgeois form, what is wealth if not the universality of individual needs, capacities, enjoyments, productive forces, etc., created in universal exchange? [What if not] the full development of human control over natural forces, both over those of the so-called nature and over his own nature? [What if not] the absolute elaboration of its creative dispositions with no other prerequisite than prior historical development, which makes this total plenitude of development the objective, that is, the development of all human forces as such, not measured with a preset pattern? [What, but an elaboration as a result of] which man does not reproduce himself in the determined character of him but produces his total fullness  From him [30 ] ?

Capitalism, which is based on the alienation between the labor force of the means of production, for which the former only accesses the consumption of the fruits of production by selling itself as merchandise in exchange for a salary, imposes a restricted notion of wealth. that denies any possibility of this full development of human potentialities. Saito observes that “Marx problematized this tendency of capital as the impoverishment of social wealth under the accumulation of an 'immense collection of commodities'”  [ 31 ] .

Marx also incorporated the notion of “natural wealth”, similarly threatened by the development of capitalism  [ 32 ] .

Saito observes that capitalism, which is characterized by the increasing production of commodities (the way in which, as Marx points out at the beginning of Capital , wealth "presents itself" in this mode of production) and which is presented by its apologists as a locomotive production of abundance, is characterized at the same time by certain forms of production of scarcity. Scarcity in capitalism is different from that of the societies that preceded it, in the sense that "it is a social scarcity"  [ 33 ] .

This social scarcity is also “artificial” because the wealth of social and natural wealth was originally abundant in the sense that it was valueless and accessible to community members. Scarcity must be created by the total destruction of the commons, even if this creates a disastrous situation for many in an economic and ecological sense. Lauderdale provided cases where edibles were intentionally wasted and arable land was wasted deliberately, so that market supply could be constrained to keep prices for staples high. Here the fundamental tension between wealth and merchandise is manifested, and this is the “paradox of wealth” that marks the historical peculiarity of the capitalist system  [34 ] .

For Saito, abundance of wealth and degrowth communism can be compatible, because it is a negation of wealth in the restricted sense that capitalism allows. It is about opening a horizon that makes it possible to achieve social and natural wealth in a broader sense, which is denied by capitalist commodification.

Once the artificial scarcity of capitalism is overcome, people, now freed from the constant pressure to earn money from the expanding common wealth, would have an attractive option to work less without worrying about the degradation of their quality of life. [...] Without market competition and the never-ending pressure for capital accumulation, free-associated labor and cooperative production could possibly reduce the working day to just three to six hours. Only then will people have enough time for non-consumer activities such as leisure, exercise, study and love. In other words, it is possible to reduce the scope of need not by increasing the productive forces, but by rehabilitating communal luxury, [ 35 ] .

Saito is correct when he points out that a central critique of Marx's to the capitalist mode of production is found in the impoverishment that he imposes on the labor force by establishing an alienated relationship with it, as merchandise, and forcing it to put itself at the service of capital to sustain the economy. constant wheel of accumulation. The dynamics of production for production's sake, which aims at the maximum possible or socially tolerable extension of working time in pursuit of valorization, denies all the possibilities of developing social wealth in the broad sense stated in the quote that we reproduce. above the Grundrisse. In the same way, this dynamic devastates the wealth of nature. Laying the foundations for the recovery of a broader notion of wealth, which can only begin through the capitalist “expropriation of the expropriators” to initiate a reorganization of social production with other criteria, is a nodal point of Marx's criteria.

It is also correct to suggest that Marx aimed at establishing a balanced metabolism between society and nature, as can be seen in Capital :

Just as the savage must struggle with nature to satisfy his needs, to preserve and reproduce his life, so must the civilized man, and must do so in all forms of society and under all possible modes of production. With his development, this realm of natural necessity expands, because his needs expand; but at the same time the productive forces that satisfy them are expanded. Freedom in this field can only consist in the fact that socialized man, the associated producers, rationally regulate his metabolism with nature, placing it under his collective control, instead of being dominated by him as if by a blind power; that they carry it out with the minimum use of forces and under the most dignified and appropriate conditions for his human nature. But this always remains a realm of necessity. Beyond it begins the development of human forces, considered as an end in itself, the true realm of freedom, which however can only flourish on that realm of necessity as its foundation. The reduction of working hours is the basic condition [ 36 ] .

Although Marx did not go too far into prefiguring communist society as such, beyond the sketches of how he thought about the beginnings of the transition towards it, we can agree with Saito that he understood it as a formation characterized by a rather stationary reproduction. That is, opposed to the systematic expanded reproduction that characterizes capitalism. The saving of work time spent on social reproduction, to gain free time, and not the increase in production, would be his goal.

Having said this, can it be said that all of the above is enough to affirm the existence of a notion of degrowth communism in Marx? As with the "epistemological break" that Saito claimed to find in the unpublished texts from 1868, in this case the evidence presented is not enough to support the case either. What Saito reinforces with his research is that Marx's communist vision was not productivist or "automated luxury communism" , as some post-capitalists interpret it today. Something that other authors had already shown, but that it is good to reinforce.

But to understand Marx as a degrowth communist is to want to rewrite his proposals in light of the discussions of the 21st century and in accordance with the proposals in vogue. Currently, although the positions of technological fetishism criticized by Saito dominate on the one hand, the notion that “degrowth is the only way out of the climate emergency has also become widespread. As its name indicates, degrowth maintains that the only way to meet the goals of reducing gas emissions and achieve a sustainable perspective is to reduce the scale of production, drastically changing the patterns of production and consumption. A problematic aspect is that its emphasis on a technical aspect or an economic goal, instead of aiming at social relations, tends to make it an abstract statement. Summarizing the debates on degrowth exceeds the objectives and possibilities of this article, but for now let's say that it seems to us that degrowth, although due to its approach inevitably tends to collide with the imperatives of capitalism, and in that sense is incompatible with this way of thinking. production, however, is an approach that avoids putting strategies at the center of the issue to give a systemic response. Many of its exponents are not even anti-capitalist, let alone socialist. There are even sectors that adopt "degrowth" arguments, in a neo-Malthusian sense, arguing that the ecological emergency forces the working class and the popular sectors to "decrease" in their aspirations, as if the excessive consumption of these sectors were the root of the problem,

Saito speaks not only of degrowth, but of communism, linking it to a working class-led liquidation of the capitalist regime, which distinguishes his position from many contemporary degrowthers. The proposition of communism without growth is not new, it was already proposed by authors such as Wolfgang Harich in the 1970s. What is new about Saito is that he does not limit himself to claiming this position as his own, but rather "discovers" that Marx was, in his later years, a degrowth communist.

This, in addition to being anachronistic, seems to us that it can give a distorted idea of ​​the problems of the transition to communism as Marx thought about them. Saito fails to prove that Marx gave indications of having profoundly reconsidered the clues that he left in his texts. It would be striking that the "new Marx" that Saito claims to have found in the unpublished books would not have more clearly warned that he was carrying out such a profound reconsideration of such fundamental problems, if this had indeed occurred. The precondition to reach communism is to cross the threshold of uneven and combined development, bequeathed by capitalism. This means that, even if immediately, the working class in power can quickly apply degrowth to some completely sumptuary productions that characterize this system, and that the maximum efforts be made to put the emergency brake on the imbalances created by capitalism in the metabolism with nature, seeking a balance, investment efforts in necessary and postponed social infrastructure will be imposed in the transition, in many areas . If we think about it on a planetary level, with the inequalities and distortions imposed by the imperialist system, we can get an idea of ​​the challenges of this transition.

Although, as we said, Saito is correct in many of his criticisms of the fetishists of technological solutions, he is one-sided in emphatically dismissing the role that a greater development of the productive forces can play in a communist society, in which metabolism balanced with nature is a central objective. Saito tends to identify any increase in productivity with an increase in the volume of production, and as such rejects it, emphasizing that abundance can still be achieved by lowering productivity. But Saito turns his back on an important possibility, and that is that new, more productive technologies can continue to be developed in a communist society even if it does not always seek to produce more and more as an end in itself –as occurs in capitalism–, but with the goal of increasing the performance of work to save it. In other words, certain technological developments can be allies of a society that seeks to reduce the necessary work, as long as the goal of maintaining a rational or balanced relationship with natural metabolism is kept in mind at all times. In this sense is the quote from Engels, about knowing and applying the laws of nature, which we reproduce above, which Saito mentions negatively because he considers that it contains an idea of ​​"dominion" over the natural world.

At the same time, the "technological solutions" to the environmental problems that capitalism is leaving as a legacy to any social economic formation that succeeds it, which can be fallacious as a mitigation strategy proposed by green capitalism to continue growing unbridled or in the is that they are faced by post-capitalists with their technological fetishism, they can be part of the necessary arsenal in a society in transition to communism. Technology alone cannot be trusted to solve the upheavals of capitalist development; technology is never neutral, rather its developments depend on the society in which they are part of. But neither turn your back on the possibility of introducing,

For all this, neither communism nor the transition to it, as Marx thought and as we must think today, can be reduced to the problem or the goal of “degrowth”.

To finish, let's say that despite Saito's criticism of post-capitalist currents for their “capitalist realism”, the political roadmap he proposes is not very different in fact. In Capital in the Atropocene, Saito vindicates the experience of the municipalism of the government of Ada Colau Ballano, who since 2015 heads the Barcelona mayor's office, which he reached with the formation of the Barcelona en Comú (BC) coalition with the confluence of Iniciativa per Catalunya Verds, Esquerra Unida i Alternativa, Equo, Procés Constituent, Podemos and the Guanyem platform. For Saito, the Climate Emergency Commission is exemplary due to the broad participation of numerous organizations in the deliberations and development of proposals. In other words, one of the neo-reformist projects mentioned above. Saito mentions in this book the need to "expand democracy outside parliaments by expanding the scope of the common until it reaches the dimension of production"; [ 37 ] , all issues that appear as the creation of “common” spaces without a rupture of the capitalist regime and its State, but rather as processes that occur within its framework. Although the importance of reaching the field of production is mentioned, this does not appear to be related to a strategy for the working class to hegemonize in a perspective of profound transformations, rather the emphasis appears on "citizen assemblies" and other similar initiatives. to "renew" "parliamentary democracy" in combination with municipalism embodied by examples such as Barcelona en Comú. That is, the same “capitalist realism” that he criticized seems to pervade Saito's perspective.

Final comments

Beyond the controversies that we have raised, Saito incorporates new elements on Marx's view of the metabolism between society and nature under capitalism, and also on the reformulation of the notions of wealth, social and natural, involved in his communist perspective. Although in his search for a “new Marx” he does some debatable reading and may exaggerate some findings, it is stimulating work and brings up burning issues for discussing an ecosocialist perspective today.
